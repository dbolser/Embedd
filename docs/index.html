Pioneers without citations

Can we recognise the papers that led the way — from where they sit in the space of ideas, and when they arrived — without ever counting a citation?

The idea

Place every research abstract into a shared "map of meaning," where papers about similar things land near each other, and remember when each one was published. A genuinely foundational paper should then stand out as an early arrival in a region that only fills in later — it reaches empty ground that the field then crowds into.

If that pattern is real, we can spot foundational work from content and timing alone — with no citations. That matters, because citation counts can be gamed by self-citation and cliques, and the shape of an idea in meaning-space cannot. That's the "reckoning" the project is after: telling real pioneers apart from the bandwagon.

The surprise

The obvious guess is that pioneers are lonely outliers — papers floating far from everything else. That turns out to be wrong. Founding papers sit comfortably among their contemporaries.

What actually marks them is timing relative to their neighbours: a pioneer's conceptual neighbourhood arrives after it. A pioneer isn't a paper standing apart from the crowd — it's a paper standing at the front of a crowd that hasn't gathered yet.

Every dot is a paper. Known landmark papers (the stars) all gather in the top-right — each one leading a large field that formed behind it in time. The dense stripe on the left is derivative work that arrived into an idea already well-populated.

What it can and can't do

Can Recognise & foresee

Without seeing a single citation, it re-finds the famous landmark papers of several fields — and, standing at a past moment, it can flag which still-obscure papers are about to matter, before the citations arrive.

Can't Judge worth

It cleanly spots the opposite of a pioneer — the tools and databases that rack up citations after an idea lands. But it can't resurrect forgotten greats: arriving early isn't the same as being right. It reads leadership of a trend, not correctness.

Where it's going

The next question is bigger: does science advance through a small, reusable vocabulary of moves — "make it genome-wide," "make it single-cell" — that show up as recurring directions in the map of meaning? We're scaling the corpus up across a broad slice of the literature to find out.

Foundational papers stand out not because they stand alone, but because they arrive first where everyone later gathers.
